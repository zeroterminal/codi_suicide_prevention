GET EDUCATED

Suicide is the act of intentionally causing one’s own death. A suicide attempt is when someone harms themselves with any intent to end their life, but they do not die as a result of their actions.

Warning Signs:

Warning Signs help the visitors recognize potential and early signs in themselves or others and encourage early intervention, for example: 1. Talking about suicide: expressing the thoughts of wanting to die or hopelessness. 2. Getting the means to take your own life: such as buying a gun or stockpiling pills. 3. Having mood swings: extreme shifts in moods from being happy to being sad in a short period of time or vice-versa. 4. Isolation: stops attending social events or social gatherings with family and friends. 5. Increasing substance abuse (alcohol, drugs, etc...). 6. Loss of interest: loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities or hobbies. 7. Changing daily routine: such as eating or sleeping patterns.

Risk Factors:

Risk factors, which may be individual or environmental, can increase the likelihood of suicidal behavior. For example: 1. Attempted suicide before. 2. Feeling hopeless, worthless, or socially isolated. 3. Experiencing a stressful life event, such as the loss of a loved one, a breakup, or financial or legal problems. 4. Having suicidal thoughts and having access to firearms in your home. 5. Having an underlying psychiatric disorder, such as major depression, or post-traumatic stress disorder. 6. Having a family history of mental disorders, suicide, or violence, including physical or sexual abuse. 7. Having a medical condition that can be linked to depression and suicidal thinking, such as chronic disease or terminal illness.

Suicide Prevention Methods:

Suicide prevention methods and treatment are based on patient risk factors. Such as: 1. Speaking Up: whether the individual has suicidal thoughts, or you spot the warning signs in someone else, you must speak up. Although trust is important, keeping a person’s planned method of suicide a secret can put their life at risk. 2. Psychotherapy or talk therapy; most common: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy: individuals are taught new ways of dealing with stress and stressful life experiences instead of directing to take their own life. Dialectical Behavior Therapy: individuals are taught to recognize disruptive or unhealthy feelings or actions. 3. Increased education and awareness among medical professionals might decrease suicide rates in the future: in some cases, warning signs were dismissed by the medical professionals leading the individuals to suicide or attempt suicide. 4. Medications: some medications used in the treatment of mental disorders include an increased risk of suicide as a side effect; medications are still controversial.

OUR GOAL

  • Reduce the Suicide Attempt Rate.
  • Increase Communication efforts via all social media that promote positive messages and support safe intervention strategies.
  • Increase Knowledge of warning signs for suicide.
  • Increase knowledge of the factors that offer protection from suicidal behaviors and that promote wellness and recovery.
  • Extend care and support to individuals who are experiencing thoughts of suicide.
  • Provide care and support to individuals affected by suicide deaths and attempt to promote healing and implement community strategies to help prevent further suicides.
